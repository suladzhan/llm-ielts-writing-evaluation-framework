## SAMPLE 9 — Test 1, Task 1 — Official Band 6.0

**Task:** The graph below gives information about the percentage of the population in four Asian countries living in cities from 1970 to 2020, with predictions for 2030 and 2040. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant. Write at least 150 words.

**Visual (line graph — text description):** Title "Percentage of the population living in cities". Y-axis = percentage (%) of total population (0–90). X-axis = year (1970, 1980, 1990, 2000, 2010, 2020, 2030, 2040). Four countries:
- **Malaysia** — highest throughout and rising fastest. ~30% (1970), ~40% (1980), ~50% (1990), ~60% (2000), ~70% (2010), ~75% (2020), ~80% (2030), ~83% (2040). Dotted line = projected from 2020.
- **Philippines** — starts high but flattens. ~30% (1970), ~35% (1980), ~49% (1990), ~46% (2000), ~44% (2010), ~46% (2020), ~52% (2030), ~57% (2040).
- **Thailand** — starts low but rises. ~20% (1970), ~25% (1980), ~30% (1990), ~30% (2000), ~32% (2010), ~35% (2020), ~42% (2030), ~50% (2040).
- **Indonesia** — lowest start, strong rise. ~14% (1970), ~18% (1980), ~26% (1990), ~42% (2000), ~52% (2010), ~62% (2020), ~64% (2030) — projected. Note: Indonesia overtakes Philippines around 2010.

**Essay (Band 6.0, text as written):**

> The line graph illustrates the proportion of urban citizens in Philippines, Malaysia, Thailand and Indonesia between 1970 and 2020, with the expected population in 2030 and 2040.
>
> Overall, it can be easily seen that all four countries has a dramatic rise in population, starting at the lowest point in 1970 and being projected to reach the peak in 2040.
>
> Malaysia and Indonesia saw a slightly rise in population, respectively from 30 to about 45 and around 12 to over 20 percent from 1970 to 1990. After that, both countries has climbed rapidly until now before being predicted to continue increasing for the next 20 years.
>
> After almost remaining in the 70s, Philippines percentage increased dramatically to reach about 47 percent on 1990 and dropped to 40% in 2010. Then, this country faced slightly rise in 2020. Meanwhile, Thailand reached approximate 30% in 1990 and saw not much changes until 2020. Both two countries are projected to increase in population in 2030 and 2040.

**Per-criterion (inferred from examiner comment):**
- Task Achievement: ~6 — covers requirements, overview in second paragraph, key features identified per country; but overview states "all four countries has a dramatic rise" which misrepresents Philippines and Thailand (they are relatively flat for long periods). More detail needed from 1990 onwards.
- Coherence & Cohesion: ~7 — ideas grouped by similar trend (Malaysia+Indonesia, then Philippines+Thailand); effective linkers ("Overall", "After that", "Meanwhile"); minor error ("Both two").
- Lexical Resource: ~6 — range with some less common items ("proportion", "expected population", "dramatic rise", "projected to reach the peak"); errors ("slightly rise" / slight rise, "almost remaining" / remaining almost, "approximate" / approximately).
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy: ~6 — mix of complex forms ("being predicted to continue increasing"); errors remain but rarely reduce communication.

**Why 6.0:** CC at 7 is the strongest area; TA pulled down by inaccurate overview generalisation and missing data detail; LR and GRA at 6 due to recurring errors.

## SAMPLE 10 — Test 1, Task 2 — Examiner Model Answer (~Band 7.5–8)

**Task:** The most important aim of science should be to improve people's lives. To what extent do you agree or disagree with this statement? Write at least 250 words.

**Essay (Examiner model, near error-free):**

> Scientific developments are occurring at a great rate but some of them do not seem to be of help to people. In fact, sometimes scientific innovations are regretted by those who invented them. This essay will argue that science should never harm people but scientists should aim to further their understanding as much as to improve people's lives.
>
> On one hand, there is a strong argument that the public good should be the top priority for scientists. They are the ones who have the potential to make discoveries and invent things that can change the world. Electricity, modern medicine, telecommunications and the internet are just some of the scientific innovations that have changed lives for the better.
>
> On the other hand, sometimes scientists do research just in the hope of adding to their knowledge. While they should make absolutely sure that their experiments do no harm, they may not know until they have finished how their findings will be used and whether they will improve people's lives. The scientist Nobel invented dynamite to help with mining, not knowing that it would one day be used in weapons, and the scientist who discovered the life-saving drug penicillin did so quite by chance.
>
> Overall, it seems that science should improve the lives of people and that ought to be one of its aims. However, knowledge and discovery are aims in themselves and are just as important for scientists. Sometimes scientists do not know how their scientific breakthroughs will be used until their work is done.

**Per-criterion (inferred from examiner comment):**
- Task Response: ~8 — well-developed response, concludes that science aims should include improving lives but also furthering knowledge; nuanced position acceptable for "to what extent"; both views with examples (electricity/medicine vs dynamite/penicillin).
- Coherence & Cohesion: ~7 — logically organised with clear paragraphing; cohesive devices used ("On one hand / On the other hand / Overall / However / While / Sometimes") but linking words appear frequently at sentence starts, making it slightly mechanical.
- Lexical Resource: ~8 — natural and sophisticated ("scientific breakthroughs", "life-saving drug", "public good"); no errors noted.
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy: ~8 — wide range of structures including conditionals, relative clauses, modal verbs; rare errors only.

**Why ~7.5–8:** High-level fully developed response with sophisticated vocabulary and accurate grammar; held slightly below 8 by the mechanical/repetitive use of sentence-initial linkers.

## SAMPLE 11 — Test 2, Task 1 — Official Band 5.5

**Task:** The chart below shows the number of households in the US by their annual income in 2007, 2011 and 2015. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant. Write at least 150 words.

**Visual (grouped bar chart — text description):** Title "Number of US households (in millions), by annual income". Y-axis = number of households (millions, 0–35). X-axis = three year groups: 2007, 2011, 2015. Five income categories (bars within each year group):
- **Less than $25,000** (darkest): 2007 ~25.5m, 2011 ~29m, 2015 ~28m.
- **$25,000–$49,999**: 2007 ~27m, 2011 ~30m, 2015 ~29m.
- **$50,000–$74,999** (lightest): 2007 ~21m, 2011 ~21m, 2015 ~21m. Virtually unchanged.
- **$75,000–$99,999**: 2007 ~15m, 2011 ~14m, 2015 ~15m. Nearly unchanged.
- **$100,000 or more** (dark): 2007 ~30m, 2011 ~28m, 2015 ~33m.

Key trends: lower income groups (<$50k) rose 2007–2011 then fell back slightly by 2015; middle income ($50k–$75k) stayed flat; higher income groups ($75k+) dipped in 2011 then recovered/increased by 2015; the $100k+ group had the most notable increase overall by 2015.

**Essay (Band 5.5, text as written):**

> The graph illustrats the data of different levels of annual wage of families in the united states in 2007, 2011 and 2015.
>
> As far as the number of families whose annual income was less than $25,000, it began with around >5 million in 2007, then increased to approximately 27 million in 2011, but decreased by 2 million 4 years later. Similarly, those of families that earned $25,000 to $49,999 were about 25 million in 2007, after that it rose to nearly 30 million in 2011. Finally it decreased to around 27 million in 2015.
>
> By contrast, the groups of families which have high income, including $75,000 to $99,999 and $100,000 or more, experienced a drop in 2011, but their data went up in 2015.
>
> Interesting things could be seen in the group of middle income, the data remain the same in 2007, 2011 and 2015.

**Per-criterion (inferred from examiner comment):**
- Task Achievement: ~5 — presents most data, key features covered; but NO overview of the information. Examiner note: an overview is needed (e.g. lower-income households rose then fell; higher-income dipped then increased; mid-level remained stable).
- Coherence & Cohesion: ~6 — information arranged coherently (lower income, then higher, then mid); some effective cohesive devices ("As far as", "Similarly", "By contrast"); minor errors.
- Lexical Resource: ~6 — suitable vocabulary for the task; one notable error (">5 million" instead of "25 million" — a significant data reporting error).
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy: ~5 — mix of simple and complex structures; missing capitalisation ("united states"), tense error ("remain" / "remained").

**Why 5.5:** Absence of overview caps TA at 5; CC and LR at 6; GRA at 5. Averages to 5.5.

## SAMPLE 12 — Test 2, Task 2 — Examiner Model Answer (~Band 7.5–8)

**Task:** Some university students want to learn about other subjects in addition to their main subjects. Others believe it is more important to give all their time and attention to studying for a qualification. Discuss both these views and give your own opinion. Write at least 250 words.

**Essay (Examiner model, near error-free):**

> Students attend university to improve their prospects and find suitable employment after graduation. For this reason, some feel that they should focus all their energy on their main subjects to gain a relevant qualification. Others want a more well-rounded education, so they try to learn about additional subjects.
>
> It is perfectly reasonable for students to enter university with a strong sense of curiosity and a desire to learn as much as possible. Unfortunately, we tend to put subjects into artificial boxes, suggesting that business, art and science are not connected. If students become too focused on a single area, it may stifle their initial curiosity, limiting their potential. They could also graduate with a very narrow skill set that doesn't translate well to the current job market, which often favours those who have taken a multidisciplinary approach to their studies.
>
> Despite this, caution is certainly needed. The more we learn about a subject, the more complex it becomes. Gaining an in-depth, specialist knowledge of a subject requires a certain level of focus and dedication over a long period. If we try to learn about too many things at once, our knowledge may lack the depth required to obtain a qualification. If they are not careful, young people could begin to lose interest in their main subjects, which would be detrimental to their studies.
>
> While learning about other subjects is not necessarily a bad thing, I believe university students should ensure that their main subjects remain the priority so that they do not lose sight of their objective: gaining a qualification. Then they can calculate how much time, energy and headspace they have left for learning about other topics.

**Per-criterion (inferred from examiner comment):**
- Task Response: ~8 — addresses both parts of the task; clear opinion at the end; both sides developed in well-structured paragraphs ("artificial boxes" argument vs specialist depth argument); position could have been set out at the start.
- Coherence & Cohesion: ~8 — logically organised, clear central topic in each paragraph, clear progression; cohesive devices natural and varied ("For this reason", "Unfortunately", "Despite this", "While").
- Lexical Resource: ~8–9 — natural and sophisticated without errors ("stifle their initial curiosity", "multidisciplinary approach", "detrimental to their studies", "artificial boxes"); no errors noted.
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy: ~8–9 — wide range including conditionals (multiple "if" clauses), relative clauses, modal verbs, complex noun phrases; no errors noted.

**Why ~7.5–8:** High-level, fully developed, near error-free response. The only noted weakness is that the opinion is delayed to the conclusion rather than stated upfront.

## SAMPLE 13 — Test 3, Task 1 — Examiner Model Answer (~Band 7.5–8)

**Task:** The diagram below shows the floor plan of a public library 20 years ago and how it looks now. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant. Write at least 150 words.

**Visual (two floor plans — text description):** "Central Library 20 years ago" vs "Central Library today". Entrance at the bottom centre in both.

**20 years ago (layout):**
- Back-left area: CDs, videos, computer games
- Middle-left area: Adult fiction books
- Front-left area: Reading room (newspapers & magazines)
- Centre: Tables & chairs (circular arrangement)
- Back-right area: Children's books
- Middle-right area: Adult non-fiction books
- Front-right area: Enquiry desk (tickets, book returns, etc.)

**Today (layout):**
- Back-left area: Storytelling events space (with two sofas)
- Middle-left area: Children's fiction books
- Front-left area (bottom-left): Computer room
- Centre: Open space (no tables shown)
- Back-right area: Lecture room
- Middle-right area: Adult fiction books + Information desk + Self-service machines
- Front-right area: Café

**Key changes:** CDs/videos/games replaced by Storytelling events space with sofas; Children's books moved from back-right to middle-left; Adult fiction moved from middle-left to middle-right; Adult non-fiction section replaced by Information desk and self-service machines; Reading room (newspapers/magazines) replaced by Computer room; Enquiry desk replaced by Café; Lecture room added (back-right); Tables & chairs in centre removed, creating open space.

**Essay (Examiner model, near error-free):**

> The diagrams show Central Library before and after changes were made to its layout.
>
> Clearly, the library has been updated to include dedicated spaces for events and now has an open space in the centre.
>
> Twenty years ago, to the left of the entrance there was a reading room with newspapers and magazines and to the right there was an enquiry desk. Now there's a computer room on the left and a café where the information desk once was. Adult fiction has moved to the right-hand side of the room and all reference books are now on the left. There is a smaller information desk with self-service machines in what once was the adult non-fiction section.
>
> There was a room at the back left-hand corner that had computer games, CDs and videos that has since been enlarged and houses children's fiction. It has a space with two sofas where storytelling events are held. There is a lecture room in the back right-hand corner where children's books used to be.

**Per-criterion (inferred from examiner comment):**
- Task Achievement: ~7–8 — covers the main changes; overview in second paragraph (dedicated event spaces, open centre); organised by area of the library (front, then back); overview could be extended to include more key changes (self-service, café).
- Coherence & Cohesion: ~8 — ideas arranged logically by section (front changes, then back); good cohesion ("once was", "now on the left", "has since been"); clear progression.
- Lexical Resource: ~8 — good range showing flexibility and precision ("dedicated spaces", "been updated", "houses children's fiction", "enlarged"); no errors.
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy: ~8 — error-free sentences; variety in structures when describing changes ("has been updated", "what once was", "has since been enlarged").

**Why ~7.5–8:** Strong, near error-free response with clear logical organisation and good vocabulary range. Held just below 8 by the slightly general overview.

## SAMPLE 14 — Test 3, Task 2 — Examiner Model Answer (~Band 7.5–8)

**Task:** In many countries around the world, rural people are moving to cities, so the population in the countryside is decreasing. Do you think this is a positive or negative development? Write at least 250 words.

**Essay (Examiner model, near error-free):**

> Statistics show that the world's population is increasing rapidly. It is expected that most of us will be living in cities within the next few decades. The question of whether urbanisation is a positive or negative development remains controversial.
>
> A rapid influx of people moving from rural to urban areas is bound to cause problems. Firstly, pressure on resources such as housing and transportation intensifies. It is becoming difficult for many people to afford adequate housing in cities. A by-product of this is the creation of slums causing low-income families to group together in neglected parts of the city. These people often become trapped in a cycle of poverty that is difficult to escape.
>
> As mentioned above, the growth of urban areas can also lead to severe traffic congestion because more and more vehicles travel into the city from the suburbs. This has many knock-on effects, such as problems with air quality. It also leads many city dwellers to experience mental health issues because travelling across large cities is tiring and stressful.
>
> All of this being said, I don't believe that urbanisation is a wholly negative development. There are advantages to living in large cities that are well managed. For example, there are more schools which means more education opportunities. Access to higher-quality health care is often better in cities. Some cities have also introduced ride-sharing, e-bikes and park and ride services that reduce environmental problems.
>
> My opinion is that many of the problems associated with urbanisation are avoidable but dealing with increasing populations in cities is a formidable challenge. How governments, businesses and society respond to this challenge will dramatically affect the future of our world.

**Per-criterion (inferred from examiner comment):**
- Task Response: ~7 — addresses both sides of the question, presents a position (not wholly negative); well-developed paragraphs with examples; however, the part about "countryside population decreasing" is not explicitly covered — this is a noted gap that would need to be included for a full answer.
- Coherence & Cohesion: ~8 — logically organised with clear progression (problems first, then advantages, then conclusion); good cohesion ("A by-product of this", "As mentioned above", "All of this being said"); clear topic in each paragraph.
- Lexical Resource: ~8 — natural and sophisticated ("bound to cause", "trapped in a cycle of poverty", "knock-on effects", "formidable challenge"); rare minor error ("education opportunities" / "educational opportunities").
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy: ~8 — flexible and accurate, wide range of structures; some shorter sentences noted that could be extended.

**Why ~7.5–8:** High-level response with sophisticated vocabulary and grammar; TA slightly capped at 7 because the countryside depopulation dimension is not addressed.

## SAMPLE 15 — Test 4, Task 1 — Examiner Model Answer (~Band 7.5–8)

**Task:** The graph below shows the average monthly change in the prices of three metals during 2014. Summarise the information by selecting and reporting the main features, and make comparisons where relevant. Write at least 150 words.

**Visual (line graph — text description):** Title "Average monthly change in prices of copper, nickel and zinc (2014)". Y-axis = % change in price compared with previous month (-4 to +7). X-axis = months (J F M A M J J A S O N D). Three metals:
- **Nickel** (solid line with dots): starts highest at ~+6% (Jan), drops sharply to ~+4% (Feb), ~+1% (Mar-May), plunges to ~-3% (Jun), rises to ~-1% (Jul-Sep), drops to ~-2% (Oct), recovers to ~+1% (Nov-Dec).
- **Zinc** (x markers): starts ~+3% (Jan), drops to ~+2% (Feb), ~+1% (Mar-Apr), ~0% (May), ~-0.5% (Jun), fluctuates near 0 to -0.5% (Jul-Sep), drops to ~-1% (Oct), rises to ~+2% (Nov-Dec).
- **Copper** (dashed circles): starts ~+2% (Jan), holds ~+1% (Feb-May), drops to ~-0.5% (Jun), rises to ~+1% (Jul-Sep), holds ~+0.5% (Oct-Nov), rises to ~+2% (Dec). Most stable of the three.

Key feature: all three metals dip in June. Nickel is most volatile. Copper is most stable. Zinc and copper both end higher in December.

**Essay (Examiner model, near error-free):**

> From the line graph, we can see the average monthly rise or fall in how much copper, nickel and zinc cost throughout 2014.
>
> Overall, nickel started with the highest percentage change of the three but ended with the lowest. Zinc started with the smallest change and ended with the highest, and copper prices fluctuated. Prices for all three metals dipped in June.
>
> In January the price of nickel was up six per cent, but this dropped to negative three percent in June, ending the year with a one percent increase in both November and December. Copper began the year with an increase of two percent and by May, the price was dropping (by slightly less than one per cent). It rose again, increasing by a percentage point in July, August and September. Zinc saw its most dramatic increase in February, with three per cent and the price fell in June (a one percent decrease). The change was negative until October/November when it began to rise.

**Per-criterion (inferred from examiner comment):**
- Task Achievement: ~7 — intro + overview identifying key differences between metals and the June dip; main trends for each metal reported; some details missing (copper after September; no percentage values Jul-Sep for any metal).
- Coherence & Cohesion: ~7 — coherent arrangement, each metal taken in turn; good cohesive devices ("Overall", "but", "with", "when"); examiner notes more variety of cohesive devices could improve the score.
- Lexical Resource: ~7 — adequate with attempts at less common items ("fluctuated", "dramatic increase") and synonyms ("ended with the lowest", "dipped", "dropped").
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy: ~6–7 — some variety including comparatives and continuous tenses ("was dropping"); most sentences simple or compound; lack of complex structures noted.

**Why ~7–7.5:** Good overview and organisation; vocabulary and structure hold it just below the highest model level due to missing data and limited structural complexity.

## SAMPLE 16 — Test 4, Task 2 — Examiner Model Answer (~Band 7.5–8)

**Task:** In many countries, people are now living longer than ever before. Some people say an ageing population creates problems for governments. Other people think there are benefits if society has more elderly people. To what extent do the advantages of having an ageing population outweigh the disadvantages? Write at least 250 words.

**Essay (Examiner model, near error-free):**

> The population in most parts of the world is ageing; people are living longer and there are fewer younger people in many places as birth rates fall. This phenomenon has pros and cons, but this essay will contend that, on balance, the advantages of having an older population outweigh the negatives.
>
> The first issue that occurs to many people when considering the ageing population is the expense. If people live longer, they may have more than 30 years of retirement and may need to be supported financially by the government or their families. If they experience age-related illness, this impacts the health system and takes up resources needed by other people in society. What's more, when older people are financially independent, it might be hard for younger people if they have to compete to get a foot in the door of the housing market or gain employment.
>
> That being said, older people have a lot to contribute in terms of wisdom, experience and skills. Many people are active and productive for longer than their counterparts were 50 years ago and are an asset to the economy and society well into their old age. They are able to work for longer and after retirement they contribute in many ways too, such as by doing charitable work, spending money as consumers and supporting their families. Grandparents often care for their young grandchildren, making it easier for both parents to work.
>
> While it is true that an ageing population poses challenges for governments, it is clear that these are outweighed by the significant benefits that elderly people bring to society.

**Per-criterion (inferred from examiner comment):**
- Task Response: ~8 — both sides presented with ideas extended; position clear from the beginning; well-supported ideas (expense, housing market, employment vs wisdom, productivity, charitable work, childcare); conclusion is relevant and justified.
- Coherence & Cohesion: ~8 — ideas organised with good progression; clear topic in each paragraph; cohesion natural ("What's more", "That being said", "While it is true that").
- Lexical Resource: ~8 — skilfully used with high-level terms ("phenomenon", "age-related illness", "impacts the health system") and sophisticated expressions ("get a foot in the door", "counterparts").
- Grammatical Range & Accuracy: ~8 — range of complex structures including several conditionals ("If people live longer... If they experience... if they have to compete"); conditionals used accurately without errors.

**Why ~7.5–8:** High-level, fully developed response with sophisticated vocabulary and accurate grammar across all criteria.

# Notes on this batch

**Band coverage added:** This part adds Band 5.5 (Sample 11), Band 6.0 (Sample 9), and approximately Band 7.5–8 upper anchors (Samples 10, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16).

**How to use the examiner model answers:** These are not student responses — they are near-perfect examples. When a new essay is closer to these in terms of vocabulary sophistication, grammatical range, task coverage and organisation, it should be in the 7.5–8 range. Key differences from Band 7.0 (Sample 6 in Part 1): no significant errors, more natural cohesion (not mechanical), richer vocabulary with idiomatic expressions, and more fully developed ideas with specific examples.

**The Band 6.0 vs 7.0 vs 8 boundary in summary:**
- Band 6: addresses the task, adequate vocab/grammar, errors present but don't block meaning, paragraphing may be inconsistent.
- Band 7: clear position/overview throughout, effective collocation, variety of complex structures, errors minor and infrequent.
- Band 8: fully developed, sophisticated and natural vocabulary, wide and flexible grammar, errors only as rare slips.
